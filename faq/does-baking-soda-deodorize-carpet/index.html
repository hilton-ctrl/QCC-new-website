Does Baking Soda Really Deodorize Carpet?

Honest comparison of what baking soda does and doesn't fix — with a table showing which odour types respond to DIY and which need professional treatment.

97 five-star Google reviews

Short answer: Baking soda partially deodorises carpet — it works on light, surface-level odours by absorbing volatile odour molecules. It does NOT work on the most common causes of persistent carpet smell: pet urine (uric acid crystals), set-in protein residue, or embedded smoke. For those, enzyme treatment plus hot water extraction is what actually removes the source.

What baking soda actually does

Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) is mildly alkaline and slightly absorbent. When sprinkled on carpet, it works in two ways:

  • Absorbs moisture — light dampness in the carpet fibre, which can carry odour molecules
  • Neutralises some acidic odours — particularly volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from cooking smells, body oils, and mild perspiration

If your carpet smells mildly stale from regular living, vacuuming, baking soda overnight, and re-vacuuming can produce a noticeable improvement. It's the right tool for mild, recent, surface-level odour.

Where baking soda fails

Baking soda is not a treatment for:

Pet urine odour. As urine dries, it forms uric acid crystals that are insoluble in water and not broken down by sodium bicarbonate. Baking soda may absorb a small amount of moisture from a fresh accident, but it does nothing to the crystals that produce the persistent smell. This is the most common reason people try baking soda multiple times and the smell keeps returning.

Set-in food and protein spills. Milk, vomit, blood, and food protein need enzyme treatment to break down the protein structure. Baking soda cannot do this chemistry.

Smoke residue. Cigarette and bushfire smoke clings to carpet fibres at the molecular level. Baking soda absorbs minor surface residue but doesn't extract embedded smoke.

Damp or mildew odour. Until the moisture source is identified and resolved, baking soda only masks the symptom temporarily.

Embedded long-term residue. Months or years of accumulated body oils, dust, and soil in high-traffic areas need professional hot water extraction to lift out.

When to stop trying DIY

If you've tried baking soda once or twice and the smell came back within a week, the source is not at the surface. Continuing to apply baking soda will not reach the actual contamination. At that point, professional treatment is the cost-effective path — because the alternative is repeated DIY attempts that improve the smell for a few days each time and never resolve it.
