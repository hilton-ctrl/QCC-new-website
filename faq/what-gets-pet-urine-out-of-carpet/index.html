What Gets Pet Urine Out of Carpet?

Why pet urine produces uric acid crystals that DIY methods can't break down — and what the Pet Reset Protocol actually does at the source.

Short answer: Enzyme treatment gets pet urine out of carpet by breaking down the uric acid crystals that bond urine to the fibre. The enzyme converts the crystals into water-soluble compounds, which are then extracted out using hot water extraction. Standard detergent and water do not break uric acid crystals — which is why DIY treatment often fails to remove pet urine smell, even when the visible stain is gone.

Why pet urine is harder to remove than other stains

Pet urine is different from most carpet stains because it changes chemistry as it dries.

When fresh, urine is mostly water plus urea, urochrome, and various salts. At this stage, blotting with cold water and a mild detergent often removes both the visible stain and most of the smell.

As urine dries, the urea and other compounds crystallise into uric acid. Uric acid crystals are insoluble in water — meaning that no amount of water-based cleaning will dissolve them. They stay in the carpet fibre, bonded chemically. And they reactivate with humidity, which is why the pet urine smell often gets worse on hot, humid Gold Coast days even when the carpet looks dry and clean.

This is the actual mechanism behind "we cleaned the spot, but the smell keeps coming back."

What breaks down uric acid crystals

Enzyme treatment. Specifically, enzymes that target the proteins in the crystal structure and convert them into water-soluble forms.

The enzymes in professional pet urine treatment are not the same as the enzymes in supermarket "pet odour" products. Commercial-grade enzyme pre-treatment is more concentrated, has a longer dwell time built in, and is applied in volumes appropriate to the contamination depth — not as a light spray.

The process used in the Pet Reset Protocol:

  • Assess how deep the urine has penetrated (carpet fibre, pad, underlay, or subfloor)
  • Apply enzyme pre-treatment at concentration matched to the contamination
  • Allow 10–15 minutes dwell time for the enzyme to break down the crystals
  • Hot water extraction at 92°C lifts the now-soluble compounds out of the fibre
  • pH neutralisation rinse to prevent re-soiling and residue smell

What doesn't work, and why

Plain water and detergent: doesn't dissolve uric acid crystals. Cleans the visible stain but leaves the smell.

Vinegar and baking soda: mild improvements only. Vinegar is acidic but evaporates quickly; baking soda absorbs but doesn't break down crystals at the source.

Carpet shampoo machines (DIY rental): apply detergent and water but lack the vacuum power and water temperature to lift treated material out. Often leaves the pad saturated, making the problem worse.

Supermarket "pet stain remover" sprays: generally surfactant-based with light enzyme content. Sometimes works on fresh accidents (under 24 hours). Rarely works on set-in urine.
